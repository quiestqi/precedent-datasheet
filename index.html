2. Historical & Contextual Analysis

Historical Timeline

2021

AI ethics enters global policy discussion
UNESCO adopted the Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence, marking AI as a cultural, ethical, and political issue, not only a technical tool.

2022

Text-to-image AI becomes public
Stable Diffusion was publicly released, while tools such as Midjourney made AI-generated images part of everyday visual culture.

Nov 2022

ChatGPT is released
ChatGPT accelerated public debate around AI, authorship, creativity, automation, and machine-generated knowledge.

2023

AI art becomes contested
AI-generated images became part of legal and cultural debates about training data, copyright, authorship, and creative labor.

Dec 2023

Synthetic biology gains public visibility
The FDA approved Casgevy, the first FDA-approved therapy using CRISPR/Cas9 genome editing technology, making biological editing more visible in public discourse.

2023–2024

Self-contained appears
Self-contained emerges at the intersection of generative AI and synthetic biology, when both image-making and life itself were increasingly understood through computation.

Why does Self-contained appear at this moment?

Self-contained appears at a time when the boundaries between image, data, life, and material object were becoming unstable. Generative AI changed how images could be produced, circulated, and questioned, while synthetic biology changed how biological information could be edited, stored, and imagined.

Instead of using AI only to generate visual novelty, Entangled Others uses computational systems to speculate on artificial biodiversity and more-than-human forms of creativity. The project is not only about AI aesthetics; it is about the changing relationship between the digital, the biological, and the material.

What questions does it raise?

The project questions what counts as natural, artificial, biological, or computational. It challenges the boundary between living organisms and generated forms, between scientific documentation and speculative fiction, and between human creativity and machine-assisted creation.

It also asks how datasets, taxonomies, and machine vision shape the way humans classify, visualize, and imagine the living world.

Who is it for?

Self-contained is situated within contemporary art, AI art, BioArt, speculative design, and computational creativity. Its audience includes museum visitors, curators, designers, researchers, scientists, and viewers interested in ecology, technology, and more-than-human futures.

Rather than addressing only a technical audience, the work creates a space where broader publics can reconsider how technology changes the way life and nature are represented.

3. Visual & Aesthetic Representation

This layer analyzes how Self-contained uses visual language, representational strategy, material translation, and aesthetic references to construct its argument.

01

Visual Language + Representation

Self-contained uses a visual language that moves between scientific observation and speculative imagination. Its forms resemble microscopic organisms, marine life, fungi, coral, insects, and botanical structures, but they are never fully identifiable as existing species.

This ambiguity is central to the project. The viewer encounters forms that feel biological, but also artificial, computational, and unfamiliar. The project does not present nature as stable or already known; instead, it creates organisms that exist between categories: image and object, specimen and fiction, biological body and machine-generated form.

02

Aesthetic Precedents + Material Translation

The representational style draws from natural history illustration, biological taxonomy, scientific specimen display, and microscope imagery. Its detailed forms recall traditions of documenting, classifying, and displaying organisms, especially the visual language of biological diagrams and natural history archives.

At the same time, the project belongs to BioArt, speculative design, AI art, and computational creativity. Through digital video, sculpture, 3D printed components, stainless steel, and synthesized DNA, the generated image is translated into physical, digital, and biological registers.

03

Audience Experience + Argument

The viewer experiences a tension between recognition and uncertainty. The forms look familiar enough to resemble natural organisms, but unfamiliar enough to resist classification. This produces a mode of viewing that is close to both museum observation and speculative fiction.

Self-contained communicates its argument through visual ambiguity. By making the organisms appear both real and unreal, natural and artificial, familiar and alien, the project asks viewers to reconsider how life is represented, classified, and imagined in an age of generative AI and biological engineering.
