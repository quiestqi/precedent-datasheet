02

Contextual Reading

A

Why does Self-contained appear at this moment?

Self-contained appears at a moment when the boundaries between image, data, life, and material object were becoming unstable. Generative AI changed how images could be produced, circulated, and questioned, while synthetic biology changed how biological information could be edited, stored, and imagined.

Instead of using AI only to produce visual novelty, Entangled Others uses computational systems to speculate on artificial biodiversity and more-than-human creativity. The project connects digital generation with biological imagination and material production.

B

What questions does it raise?

The project questions what counts as natural, artificial, biological, or computational. It destabilizes the distinction between living organisms and generated forms, scientific documentation and speculative fiction, and human creativity and machine-assisted production.

It also asks how datasets, taxonomies, and machine vision influence the ways humans classify, visualize, and imagine the living world.

C

Who is it for?

Self-contained is situated across contemporary art, AI art, BioArt, speculative design, and computational creativity. Its audience includes museum visitors, curators, designers, researchers, scientists, and viewers interested in ecology and technology.

Rather than addressing only technical specialists, the work creates a space in which broader publics can reconsider how technology transforms representations of life and nature.

3. Visual & Aesthetic Representation

This layer analyzes how Self-contained uses visual language, representational strategy, material translation, and aesthetic references to construct its argument.

01

Visual Language + Representation

Self-contained uses a visual language that moves between scientific observation and speculative imagination. Its forms resemble microscopic organisms, marine life, fungi, coral, insects, and botanical structures, but they are never fully identifiable as existing species.

This ambiguity is central to the project. The viewer encounters forms that feel biological, but also artificial, computational, and unfamiliar. The project does not present nature as stable or already known; instead, it creates organisms that exist between categories: image and object, specimen and fiction, biological body and machine-generated form.

02

Aesthetic Precedents + Material Translation

The representational style draws from natural history illustration, biological taxonomy, scientific specimen display, and microscope imagery. Its detailed forms recall traditions of documenting, classifying, and displaying organisms, especially the visual language of biological diagrams and natural history archives.

At the same time, the project belongs to BioArt, speculative design, AI art, and computational creativity. Through digital video, sculpture, 3D printed components, stainless steel, and synthesized DNA, the generated image is translated into physical, digital, and biological registers.

03

Audience Experience + Argument

The viewer experiences a tension between recognition and uncertainty. The forms look familiar enough to resemble natural organisms, but unfamiliar enough to resist classification. This produces a mode of viewing that is close to both museum observation and speculative fiction.

Self-contained communicates its argument through visual ambiguity. By making the organisms appear both real and unreal, natural and artificial, familiar and alien, the project asks viewers to reconsider how life is represented, classified, and imagined in an age of generative AI and biological engineering.

Process Analysis

Methodology

01

Bio-Inspired Computational Method

The project uses biological structures and evolutionary processes as references for computational generation. Instead of reproducing existing organisms directly, the system develops speculative forms through algorithmic variation, growth, and transformation.

02

Data-Based Mutation and Reconstruction

Biological images and visual datasets are processed, reorganized, and mutated through generative systems. The resulting forms do not function as scientific representations but as reconstructed possibilities of synthetic life.

03

Human-in-the-Loop Curation

The computational system does not operate independently. The artists select, evaluate, and refine generated outcomes, creating an iterative relationship between machine variation and human aesthetic judgment.

04

DNA Encoding and Material Fabrication

Selected digital forms are translated into physical and biological formats through synthesized DNA, three-dimensional fabrication, and sculptural assembly. The process connects computational information with material objects and biological storage.

05 Theoretical Interpretation

Relational and More-than-Human Framework

The project can be understood through a relational and more-than-human framework. No single actor independently produces the work. Artists, datasets, compressed files, neural networks, biological metaphors, synthetic DNA, fabrication systems, galleries, and audiences collectively shape what the project presents as artificial life.

06 Critical Reflection

Methodological Limits

The available project documentation explains the conceptual workflow, but does not fully disclose the software, programming language, neural-network architecture, dataset scale, or complete provenance of the source images. This limits the extent to which the method can be technically reproduced or critically evaluated.

6. Rhetorical Analysis

Self-contained does not simply use artificial intelligence to imitate biological forms. It constructs an argument about the relationship between life and computation by treating digital images as mutable structures capable of compression, recombination, damage, reconstruction, and material storage. Its rhetorical force comes from aligning conceptual language, technical process, visual form, and physical materials around the same question: how does digital information begin to resemble a living system?

01 · Central Argument

Life and computation are presented as entangled systems.

Self-contained argues that biological life and computational information should not be understood as completely separate systems. Both operate through processes of encoding, mutation, recombination, transmission, and transformation.

Digital images are presented not as fixed visual objects, but as structures that change as they are copied, compressed, spliced, damaged, and reconstructed. By organizing image production through procedures associated with biological evolution, the project suggests that digital culture can also be understood as an unstable and evolving ecology.

The work does not claim that a digital image is literally alive. Instead, it uses similarities between biological and computational processes to destabilize conventional definitions of nature, technology, authorship, and life.

02 · Rhetorical Strategies

How the project makes its argument persuasive

02.1
Biological Metaphor

The project uses terms such as genome, mutation, recombination, grafting, and error correction to describe the transformation of digital images. These metaphors make abstract computational processes more legible by connecting them to familiar models of reproduction and evolution. The image file is framed not simply as data, but as a structure capable of inheritance and transformation.

02.2
Operational Metaphor

The biological metaphor is translated into the actual production process through data splicing, computational mutation, decoding, error correction, and neural reconstruction. The project becomes persuasive because its method performs the same processes that its conceptual argument describes. Mutation is not only represented visually; it becomes an operational procedure.

02.3
Material Enactment

In self-contained 009.x , the completed digital work is compressed, encoded into synthetic DNA, placed inside a metal capsule, and embedded within a sculptural container. The project therefore materially enacts the relationship it describes. Synthetic DNA functions as both a storage medium and rhetorical evidence that digital information can occupy a biological material form.

02.4
Scientific Visual Authority

The project draws on the visual language of microscopy, biological specimens, genetic research, natural-history display, and scientific classification. Its generated forms resemble organisms that might be observed in a laboratory or museum archive, although they cannot be identified as existing species. This scientific aesthetic gives the speculative organisms a sense of credibility.

03 · The Paradox of “Self-contained”

An apparently autonomous object produced by an extensive network

The title Self-contained suggests independence, enclosure, and self-sufficiency. In the sculptural works, the digital file is physically contained inside synthetic DNA, a capsule, and a fabricated object. The title therefore describes the material condition of the work: the information appears to be stored entirely within the sculpture.

However, the work is not truly self-contained. Its existence depends on source images, datasets, compression formats, neural networks, artificial-life simulations, artistic decisions, DNA synthesis, industrial fabrication, galleries, and audiences. What appears to be an autonomous object is actually the outcome of an extensive network of human and nonhuman actors.

Rather than proving that an artwork can exist independently, the project reveals how every apparently autonomous object depends on relationships, infrastructures, and systems that extend beyond its physical boundaries.

The sculpture contains the work, but the work cannot contain the network that produced it.
04 · Audience Positioning

Visual attraction precedes technical explanation.

Viewers first encounter detailed and unfamiliar forms resembling coral, fungi, cells, marine organisms, botanical structures, or internal organs. These forms are familiar enough to appear biological, but unfamiliar enough to resist classification. Only afterward does the project reveal the computational and material systems through which this impression was constructed.

05 Critical Position

Critique through destabilizing categories

The criticality of Self-contained does not come from directly rejecting artificial intelligence or technological development. Instead, the work destabilizes the separation between natural and artificial systems, gives digital images characteristics associated with mutation and inheritance, and challenges the idea of the artist as an independent creator. Artificial life is presented not as a single object, but as an effect collectively produced by a network.

06 Critical Tensions

Ontological critique, infrastructural limits

Although the project proposes a more-than-human understanding of creativity, its outcomes remain shaped by human selection. Its biological metaphors may also naturalize digital systems whose circulation is controlled by platforms, markets, institutions, and algorithmic power. The work makes relationships between digital and biological systems visible, while leaving energy use, material extraction, data labor, industrial fabrication, and DNA synthesis infrastructure relatively hidden. Its critique is therefore strongest at the level of ontology and representation, and less direct at the political and infrastructural level.

From Organism to Ecology

01

Generating Artificial Organisms

Artificial Remnants

Entangled Others’ earlier practice focused on generating speculative organisms through artificial intelligence. In Artificial Remnants , fictional insects are presented through images, taxonomic descriptions, sound, and 3D-printed forms.

The project asks how computation can imagine biological forms that do not exist in nature.

How can AI generate the appearance of life?

02

Modelling Life as Information

Self-contained

Self-contained continues the artists’ interest in artificial organisms, but shifts attention from their appearance to the processes through which they are produced. Mutation, recombination, encoding, and error correction become computational methods for transforming digital images.

By translating the digital work into synthetic DNA and sculpture, the project examines how information might mutate, persist, and acquire material form.

How can the informational logic of life transform and store digital images?

03

From Organism to Ecology

Liquid Strata

In later projects such as Liquid Strata , Entangled Others moves toward situated ecological systems. Scientific observations, marine data, artificial-life simulations, and material installations are combined to examine oceanic processes and relationships.

The focus expands from the transformation of a digital object to the interactions between organisms, environments, scientific instruments, and ecological data.

How can computation represent an ecological system composed of biological, environmental, and technological relations?

PART 03 · PERSONAL POSITION

Personal Assessment

An appraisal of the project’s conceptual achievements, theoretical tensions, and unresolved limitations.

01

From Artificial Life
to Informational Life

Self-contained is most compelling not because it produces convincing images of artificial organisms, but because it examines how the concept of life is reconstructed through information. Mutation, inheritance, recombination, and error correction are translated from biological processes into computational operations, while the final digital image is encoded into synthetic DNA and stored inside a sculptural object.

This movement across image, data, DNA, and sculpture gives the project conceptual coherence. Its visual language, computational method, and material construction all participate in the same argument: life and information are increasingly understood through shared systems of encoding, transmission, and transformation.

However, this also produces the project’s central theoretical tension. The work risks reducing life to information. Biological life is not merely a code that can be stored or transmitted; it is also metabolic, relational, situated, and dependent on an environment. The synthetic DNA inside the sculpture stores information, but it does not grow, reproduce, adapt, or participate in an ecosystem.

The project therefore does not create artificial life so much as it creates a material archive of the idea of artificial life.

02

Materialization and
Informational Reduction

One of the project’s strongest achievements is its movement beyond screen-based AI imagery. By encoding a digital work into synthetic DNA and placing it inside a metal sculpture, Self-contained gives digital information a material presence. It challenges the assumption that computational images are immaterial and reveals that digital culture always depends on physical substrates, storage systems, and technical processes.

Yet this materialization is selective. The sculpture makes DNA, capsules, and metal visible, while the larger infrastructure of production remains outside the frame. Computing systems, energy consumption, image datasets, laboratory services, industrial fabrication, and human labour are all necessary to produce the work, but they are not materially represented within it.

The object therefore appears materially complete while externalizing many of the systems that make it possible.

The work contains its encoded file, but it cannot contain the infrastructure, labour, and relationships through which it was produced.

03

Distributed Agency,
Unequal Authority

The project productively challenges the idea of the artist as an entirely autonomous creator. Its forms emerge through interactions among source images, compressed data, algorithms, neural networks, random mutations, synthetic DNA, fabrication systems, institutions, and audiences.

This distributed process supports a relational and more-than-human interpretation of artistic production. The work is not generated by a single authorial intention, but through a network of human and nonhuman actors.

However, distributed agency does not mean equal agency.

The artists still determine the dataset, establish the computational procedures, select the outputs, define the exhibition context, and decide which generated forms become completed artworks. Data and algorithms influence the results, but they do not possess the same authority as artists, curators, and institutions.

The project expands authorship beyond the individual artist, but does not fully redistribute control over representation.

04

Scientific Authority and
Aesthetic Seduction

Self-contained draws heavily on the visual and conceptual authority of science. Its references to DNA, mutation, biological classification, microscopy, and synthetic biology give its speculative forms a sense of plausibility. Viewers are encouraged to encounter them not simply as fictional images, but as possible organisms or scientific specimens.

This strategy makes the project visually and rhetorically effective. Its forms are sufficiently familiar to appear biological, yet sufficiently unfamiliar to resist classification.

At the same time, the project’s aesthetic refinement can obscure the limits of its scientific claims. Its biological language is persuasive, but the work does not provide the level of technical transparency expected from scientific research. The dataset, software environment, neural-network architecture, and complete production process remain only partially visible.

The project occupies an ambiguous position between scientific inquiry and scientific aesthetics.

05

Final Position

Self-contained succeeds as a material and ontological investigation into how the boundaries among image, information, biology, and computation are being reorganized. Its most significant contribution is not the invention of a new artificial organism, but the construction of a system in which digital images can be treated as mutable, inheritable, and materially stored entities.

Its critical strength lies in destabilizing categories: natural and artificial, image and organism, code and matter, author and system.

Its limitation is that it questions these categories more effectively than it questions the infrastructures and power relations behind them. The project makes informational transformation visible, but leaves data provenance, technological labour, environmental cost, and institutional authority comparatively hidden.

Self-contained is strongest as a work of speculative ontology rather than infrastructural critique.

The work makes digital information material, but it also reveals the danger of mistaking the storage of life’s code for life itself.
